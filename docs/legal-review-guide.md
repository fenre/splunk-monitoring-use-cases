# Legal review guide (Phase 4.5 QA gate)

> **Status:** active from Phase 4.5 onwards.
> **Audience:** project maintainers coordinating legal review, and
> the lawyers they work with.
> **What this is not.** This document is **not** legal advice. It is
> a process document describing how the project surfaces regulatory
> claims to legal counsel for review and how those reviews are
> recorded. See [`LEGAL.md`](../LEGAL.md) §1 for the full disclaimer.

---

## 1. Scope of legal review

Legal review covers the **regulatory claim surface** of the
repository: clause citations, primer summaries, evidence-pack claims,
and any rationale that interprets what a regulator intends. It does
**not** cover:

- SPL correctness or Splunk configuration (that is engineering and
  SME review).
- Mapping assurance levels (that is the peer-review rubric in
  `docs/peer-review-guide.md`).
- Licence compliance for third-party ingested content (that is
  already covered by `LEGAL.md` §3).

### 1.1 Content that triggers legal review

Legal review is **required** for:

- New or changed clause citations for any **tier-1 regulation**:
  GDPR, UK GDPR, PCI DSS, HIPAA, SOX/ITGC, SOC 2, ISO 27001, NIST
  CSF, NIST 800-53, NIS2, DORA, CMMC.
- New or changed prose in:
  - `docs/regulatory-primer.md` sections for tier-1 regulations.
  - `docs/evidence-packs/**` (all tier-1 evidence packs).
  - `LEGAL.md` itself.
  - `data/regulations.json` for any tier-1 regulation's metadata,
    `clauseGrammar`, `commonClauses[].description`, or
    `publicationUrl`.
- Any assertion that a UC **"satisfies"** a regulatory clause. A
  `mode: "satisfies"` claim in a UC sidecar is a stronger claim than
  `mode: "detects-violation-of"` and warrants closer legal attention.

### 1.2 Content that does **not** trigger legal review

- New UCs for **tier-2 or tier-3 regulations** (CCPA, nFADP, LGPD,
  APPI, PIPEDA, POPIA, AU Privacy, KVKK, NIST 800-171, NIST SSDF,
  FedRAMP, CMMC Level 2 unless involved with a tier-1 overlay,
  IEC 62443, NERC CIP, TISAX, CSA CCM, SWIFT CSCF, etc.).
- Derivative regulation entries auto-generated by
  `scripts/generate_phase3_3_derivatives.py`. The derivation is
  mechanical and documented; the parent regulation was already
  reviewed.
- Mini-category content (22.35–22.49) that is a cross-cutting
  technical detection with no tier-1 clause citations.
- CIM-model alignment, dashboard CSS, API surface changes,
  build-pipeline changes, CI changes.

### 1.3 Derivative content caveat

Where UK GDPR content is propagated from GDPR under the `identity`
inheritance mode, legal review does **not** repeat the GDPR review;
the derivative is equivalent by schema construction. Legal review
**is** repeated if the `clauseMapping` between parent and derivative
changes (e.g. if UK GDPR diverges from EU GDPR after Brexit
secondary legislation).

## 2. How to request a legal review

The project does not currently employ in-house counsel. Until it
does, legal reviews are requested from the organisation's own legal
function **at the time the catalogue is adopted**. The request
workflow is:

1. The PR author applies the `needs-legal-review` label on the PR.
2. The PR author fills in the **"Phase 4.5 QA gate — legal review"**
   section of the pull-request template. That surfaces the scope
   (UC IDs, clause IDs, regulations) for counsel in a consistent
   format.
3. The reviewer pings counsel (email, internal ticket, outside-counsel
   form — whatever the organisation uses). The PR blocks on the
   review.
4. Counsel responds with **approve**, **approve-with-revisions**, or
   **reject**. The response is recorded in
   `data/provenance/legal-review-signoffs.json` using the schema in
   `schemas/legal-review-signoff.schema.json`.
5. `scripts/audit_legal_review_signoffs.py` validates the recorded
   signoff. CI will fail the PR if the label is applied but the
   signoff file has no matching record.

### 2.1 What counsel needs to do

Counsel is asked to check, in priority order:

1. **Clause-number accuracy.** Does the clause cited exist in the
   published regulation text, at the version specified in
   `data/regulations.json.publicationUrl`?
2. **Mode appropriateness.** `satisfies` is a strong claim — does
   the cited SPL actually satisfy the obligation (typically a
   process-evidence control like `record-keeping`), or should it be
   downgraded to `detects-violation-of`?
3. **Primer accuracy.** The primer paragraph for the regulation does
   not misstate the obligation, scope, or sanction.
4. **Jurisdiction scope.** Regulatory text that only applies in
   certain jurisdictions (e.g. CCPA applies only to California
   "consumers" as defined in §1798.140) is clearly scoped.
5. **Effective-date correctness.** `applicableFrom` and
   `supersededBy` fields in `data/regulations.json` match the
   regulator's own effective-date declarations.

Counsel is **not** asked to check:

- Whether the SPL actually works. That is SME review.
- Whether a given organisation is subject to a regulation. That is a
  deployment-time determination per-customer.
- Whether the UC, as deployed, is *sufficient* for compliance. That
  is an audit determination, not a mapping-correctness question.

### 2.2 Timeline expectations

Legal reviews are not fast. The project's CI does not impose a
timeout; the PR blocks until counsel responds. Maintainers should
plan at least a 5 business day window for tier-1 content changes.
For urgent security-driven changes, two paths exist:

- **Split the PR.** Land the SPL and technical content immediately
  (no regulatory claims), and land the clause-citation updates in a
  separate PR that can block on legal review.
- **Escalate to project lead.** The project lead can mark content as
  `status: "draft"` in the UC sidecar; draft content is excluded
  from the released coverage numbers and from evidence packs.

## 3. What the legal review produces

A legal review produces exactly one signoff record, appended to
`data/provenance/legal-review-signoffs.json`. The record has five
outcomes:

| Outcome                  | Effect on PR                                                        |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `approved`               | PR unblocks. Signoff records reviewer name and date.                |
| `approved-with-revisions`| PR must be updated with the requested revisions before unblocking. Reviewer signs off on the revised commit. |
| `rejected`               | PR reverts the regulatory-claim change. UC may be reauthored as `contributing` assurance to avoid the contested claim. |
| `scope-downgrade`        | PR lands but the regulatory claim is softened (e.g. `mode: "satisfies"` → `mode: "detects-violation-of"`). |
| `conditional`            | PR lands with a `legalCaveat` field added to the UC sidecar spelling out counsel's caveat (see §4). |

### 3.1 Legal caveats in UC sidecars

When counsel gives a `conditional` or `approved-with-revisions`
sign-off that requires a caveat to appear alongside the claim, the
maintainer adds a `legalCaveat` string to the relevant
`compliance[]` entry. The schema (`schemas/uc.schema.json`) already
permits this field and it is rendered in the compliance scorecard
and in the generated Splunk app help text. Example:

```json
{
  "regulation": "GDPR",
  "version": "2016/679",
  "clause": "Art. 32",
  "mode": "satisfies",
  "assurance": "partial",
  "rationale": "Logs access-control decisions that implement Art. 32(1)(b) ability to ensure confidentiality.",
  "legalCaveat": "This mapping addresses Art. 32(1)(b) only. Art. 32(1)(a) pseudonymisation is out of scope for this UC; see UC-22.14.3."
}
```

## 4. How to record a sign-off

The signoff file is append-only. To add a record, edit
`data/provenance/legal-review-signoffs.json` directly and commit the
change on the same PR that contains the reviewed content.

Schema: `schemas/legal-review-signoff.schema.json`.

Example record:

```json
{
  "pr": "#123",
  "date": "2026-04-20",
  "commit": "abc1234",
  "reviewer": "Counsel A. Example, Acme LLP",
  "reviewerRole": "external-counsel",
  "scope": {
    "regulations": ["GDPR"],
    "ucs": ["22.17.1", "22.17.2"],
    "documents": ["docs/regulatory-primer.md#gdpr"]
  },
  "outcome": "approved-with-revisions",
  "revisionsRequested": [
    "Art. 30 mapping on 22.17.2 downgraded from satisfies to detects-violation-of; Splunk logs do not constitute the Art. 30 record itself but evidence its maintenance."
  ],
  "caveats": [],
  "notes": "Reviewed against GDPR 2016/679 consolidated text as of 2026-03-01."
}
```

Fields:

- `pr` — GitHub PR number with leading `#`, or `"direct-commit"`.
- `date` — ISO-8601 date counsel responded.
- `commit` — short SHA of the commit counsel reviewed. If counsel
  reviewed pre-revision content, record the commit **after**
  revisions were applied and counsel confirmed acceptance.
- `reviewer` — counsel's name and firm/role. Full name is intentional
  — the signoff file is checked into public history and auditors
  want an attributable review chain.
- `reviewerRole` — `internal-counsel`, `external-counsel`, or
  `paralegal` (paralegal sign-off is permitted for clause-number
  accuracy but not for primer or evidence-pack interpretation).
- `scope` — what counsel reviewed: tier-1 `regulations[]`, affected
  UC IDs, and documents touched.
- `outcome` — one of the five values in §3.
- `revisionsRequested[]` — required when outcome is
  `approved-with-revisions`; describes the changes counsel required.
- `caveats[]` — required when outcome is `conditional`; lists the
  caveats that must appear in `legalCaveat` fields on UC sidecars.
- `notes` — free text. Typically records the regulation version/date
  counsel consulted.

## 5. Privacy of counsel's identity

Counsel names are recorded in the public signoff file. Reviewers who
prefer not to be named publicly may instead be recorded as
`"<Firm Name> — Senior Associate"` or similar; the concrete
individual name is kept in an internal (non-public) sign-off ledger
maintained by the project lead. The public signoff must still allow
an external auditor to contact the firm and verify the review.

## 6. Relationship to SME sign-off (Phase 5.2)

Legal review and SME review are **distinct gates** and always run
in this order: peer → legal → SME.

- Legal review (this guide) covers clause citations, primer prose,
  and evidence-pack claims. Blocks merge on `rejected`;
  `approved-with-revisions` gates the final commit; `conditional`
  lands with `legalCaveat` fields added to the UC sidecar.
- SME review (Phase 5.2; see [`docs/sme-review-guide.md`](sme-review-guide.md)
  and recorded in `data/provenance/sme-signoffs.json`) covers
  technical correctness of the SPL against the authoring data
  source, Splunk compatibility, and the regulation's practical
  evidence expectations (retention, redaction, queryability, chain
  of custody). Blocks merge on `rejected`; `conditional` lands with
  `smeCaveat` fields added to the UC sidecar.

A PR that claims `compliance[].assurance == "full"` against a
tier-1 regulation passes through **all three** gates before merge:
peer review (engineering), legal review (this guide), SME review
(SPL + auditor-evidence). The three gates are sequential; if legal
review downgrades a UC to `partial` or `contributing`, SME review
is no longer required for that UC.

## 7. Historical baseline

Content authored before Phase 4.5 was landed without explicit legal
sign-offs; that content is grandfathered and does not block merges
retroactively. The baseline cut-off is the commit that introduces
`data/provenance/legal-review-signoffs.json`. New edits to
grandfathered content **do** trigger the gate.
