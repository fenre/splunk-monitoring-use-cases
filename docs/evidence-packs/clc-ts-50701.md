## 1. Purpose of this evidence pack

CLC/TS 50701:2021 is the CENELEC Technical Specification for railway-applications cybersecurity, published August 2021. It is the railway sector's primary risk-based cybersecurity standard, building on IEC 62443<sup class="ref">[<a href="#ref-6">6</a>]</sup> zone-and-conduit modelling, IEC 62443-3-3 Foundational Requirements and System Security Requirements, and IEC 62443-4-2 Component Security Requirements. CLC/TS 50701 covers the entire lifecycle of rail systems-under-consideration (SuC) including signalling, command and control, communications, rolling stock, ATP/ATO/ATC, ETCS Levels 1-3, CBTC, GSM-R, FRMCS, interlocking, traffic-management systems, and station operational technology. The standard requires every rail operator and infrastructure manager to maintain a documented Cybersecurity Management System (CSMS), identify and protect zones and conduits at the appropriate Target Security Level (SL-T), coordinate cybersecurity with EN 50126/8/9/50657 safety standards, manage supplier cyber deliverables, and operate continuous obligations including patch management with safety-case coordination, vulnerability handling, incident response, training, and annual self-assessment. The standard is forward-aligned with the in-development IEC 63452 Railway applications - Cybersecurity, which will supersede CLC/TS 50701 with refinements but preserves the clause structure. CLC/TS 50701 is increasingly enforced by national rail-cyber regulators (ANSSI, BSI, NCSC, ENISA) and is the de-facto baseline for NIS2 transport-sector compliance in the EU.

## 2. Scope and applicability

Applies to all rail operators, rail infrastructure managers, rolling-stock manufacturers, signalling-equipment suppliers, and rail-component suppliers operating within the EU/EEA, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and any jurisdiction that has adopted CENELEC technical specifications. Includes both passenger rail (heavy rail, metro, light rail, tram) and freight rail. Includes mainline, urban, suburban, and high-speed rail. Includes safety-critical rail-OT (signalling, interlocking, ATP, ATO, ATC, ETCS, CBTC, RBC, GSM-R, FRMCS, on-board signalling, trackside signalling) and rail-business systems (passenger information, ticketing, station-management, depot-management, rolling-stock-asset-management). Carve-outs: heritage / preservation railways, fairground / amusement-park rail, mining rail (covered by mining regulations).

**Territorial scope.** European Union and European Economic Area (mandatory under NIS2 for transport-sector operators of essential services / OES). United Kingdom (adopted via NCSC and the Office of Rail and Road / Department for Transport). Switzerland (adopted via FOT / BAV). International reach where rail operators in non-EU jurisdictions voluntarily adopt the standard, or where rail-component suppliers must demonstrate compliance to export into EU markets. Forward-aligned with IEC 63452 for global reach once published.

### 5.3 Evidence integrity expectations

Regulators increasingly cite **evidence-integrity failures** as aggravating factors in enforcement actions. Cross-regulation baseline expectations:

- Time-stamped, tamper-evident storage (WORM, cryptographic chaining, or append-only indexes).
- Chain-of-custody for any evidence removed from the SIEM / production system for audit or legal purposes.
- Synchronised clocks (NTP stratum ≤ 3 or equivalent) across all in-scope sources so timeline reconstruction is defensible.
- Documented retention enforcement — not just retention policy — so that deletion is auditable.

See cat-22.35 "Evidence continuity and log integrity" for UCs that implement these controls.

## 6. Control testing procedures

CLC/TS 50701 is administered by national rail-cyber regulators (ANSSI in France, BSI in Germany, NCSC in UK) and by the European Union Agency for Railways (ERA). External assurance is delivered by accredited rail-cyber assessors aligned with ISA Secure or TÜV certification regimes. NIS2 Article 21 risk-management measures and Article 23 incident-reporting obligations are enforced at the national level by NSAs / NSBs through inspections, on-demand evidence production, and post-incident enquiry. Inspections are typically conducted in two ways: (a) planned audits aligned to fiscal year and reviewing the annual self-assessment package; and (b) post-incident enquiry following a NIS2-reportable cyber incident. External assurance: many operators engage an accredited rail-cyber assessment body (TÜV SÜD Rail, RINA, Bureau Veritas, DNV) for the annual self-assessment review. Penetration testing of rail-OT is encouraged but performed under strict change-control and with safety-case review under EN 50126; black-box red-team engagements are rare due to safety implications. ISA Secure certification of components is increasingly required by procurement specifications. Continuous Splunk-based evidence collection through the UCs in this catalogue is the practical model for inspection-readiness between certification cycles.

**Reporting cadence.** Annual self-assessment to NSA/NSB on request and on the anniversary of CSMS approval. CSMS review at least every 24 months. Zone-and-conduit risk assessment refresh every 24 months or on material change. NIS2 Article 23 incident reporting: early warning within 24 hours, incident notification within 72 hours, intermediate update on request, final report within 1 month. ERA Single Rules Database submissions per direction. Quarterly rail-cyber governance committee. Annual cyber-IR exercise with safety coordination. Daily/weekly/monthly CSMS continuous-operations checks. Continuous monitoring of vulnerability handling, patch coordination, maintenance posture, supplier deliverables, and incident detection.

## 10. Enforcement and penalties

CLC/TS 50701 itself is a technical specification and is not directly enforceable; enforcement is via NIS2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) Article 32 and 33, which authorize administrative fines up to EUR 10 million or 2 % of worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher) for essential entities, and up to EUR 7 million or 1.4 % of worldwide annual turnover for important entities. National rail-cyber laws may impose parallel penalties (e.g. ANSSI under LPM in France, BSI under IT-SiG 2.0 in Germany). Safety-related cyber failures may trigger criminal liability under national rail-safety laws if a cyber incident contributes to a safety incident (rail accident, derailment, signalling failure). EN 50126/8/9/50657 safety-case violations resulting from cyber controls deployed without ISA concurrence may invalidate the safety certification, requiring re-certification of the SuC. Reputational consequences for rail operators are amplified by NSA / NSB inspection reports, ERA Single Rules Database entries, and public-safety media coverage following a rail-cyber incident with safety implications.
