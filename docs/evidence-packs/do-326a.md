## 1. Purpose of this evidence pack

RTCA DO-326A "Airworthiness Security Process Specification" (RTCA Inc., 6 August 2014) and the identical EUROCAE ED-202A (Edition 2, June 2014) are the joint US/EU industry standard that defines the airworthiness security certification process for civil aircraft, components, and systems. DO-326A is the process spec — it tells the applicant *what* to do; the companion standard DO-356A / ED-203A "Airworthiness Security Methods and Considerations" (2018) gives the methods (threat scenarios, risk assessment, security architecture). For aircraft already in service, DO-355A / ED-204A "Information Security Guidance for Continuing Airworthiness" (2020) extends the obligation to the operator, the Type Certificate Holder, and the Continuing Airworthiness Management Organisation (CAMO).

DO-326A is the *means of compliance* accepted by both the **FAA** (via Advisory Circular 20-186 "Airworthiness and Operational Approval for Aircraft Network Security Program (ANSP)") and the **EASA** (via Acceptable Means of Compliance AMC 20-42 "Airworthiness Information Security Risk Assessment"). For new type certificates (TCs) and supplemental type certificates (STCs) where Information Security is an issue, the FAA Aircraft Certification Service and the EASA project certification manager (PCM) require a Plan for Security Aspects of Certification (PSecAA) — the equivalent of a Plan for Software Aspects of Certification (PSAC) for DO-178C, scoped to airworthiness security.

EASA further imposes an Information Security Management System (ISMS) obligation on every approved organisation (design, production, continuing-airworthiness, operations, training, ANS, ATM/ANS, aerodrome) via **EASA Part-IS** — Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2022/1645 (organisation-level) and Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/203 (Member-State-level), with binding application from **22 February 2026**. Part-IS requires (a) an ISMS integrated into the existing safety management system, (b) personnel competence and training on cyber events, (c) information-security risk assessments, (d) detection of information-security events, (e) incident reporting within 72 hours of detection (preliminary) and 1 month (final).

The aircraft network-architecture framework underlying all of this is **ARINC 811 "Commercial Aircraft Information Security Concepts of Operation and Process Framework"** which divides aircraft systems into four trust domains: the Aircraft Control Domain (ACD), the Airline Information Services Domain (AISD), the Passenger Information & Entertainment Services Domain (PIESD), and the Passenger Owned Devices (POD) domain. The DO-326A security architecture and DO-355A continuing-airworthiness controls are anchored to these domains; no information flow from a less-trusted to a more-trusted domain is permitted without explicit security architecture justification.

For ground systems supporting connected aircraft (engineering, maintenance, dispatch, ACARS gateway, aeronautical database supply chain), **DO-391 / ED-205 "Aeronautical Information System Security (AISS) Framework Guidance"** provides equivalent guidance.

The Splunk monitoring catalogue's coverage maps directly into this multi-document regime: the catalogue produces an auditable evidence chain that the TC-holder Chief Engineer, the CAMO Manager, and the operator's Accountable Manager can hand to the FAA Aircraft Certification Office (ACO), the EASA project certification manager, the National Competent Authority (NCA) Part-IS inspector, or the IATA Operational Safety Audit (IOSA) auditor.

## 2. Scope and applicability

Applies to every applicant for a civil aircraft type certificate (TC), supplemental type certificate (STC), or major modification where Information Security is identified as an issue; to every operator of a TC- or STC-issued aircraft; to every Continuing Airworthiness Management Organisation (CAMO) under EASA Part-CAMO / FAA Part-145; and to every EU/EEA-approved aviation organisation under EASA Part-IS.

Specific scope rules:

- **DO-326A / ED-202A** (Airworthiness Security Process) — applies during type-certification / STC / major-modification projects.
- **DO-355A / ED-204A** (Continuing Airworthiness Information Security) — applies in-service, throughout the aircraft's operational life.
- **DO-356A / ED-203A** (Airworthiness Security Methods) — provides the methods used inside the DO-326A process and DO-355A continuing-airworthiness activity.
- **DO-391 / ED-205** (AISS) — applies to ground systems that connect to or support connected aircraft.
- **FAA AC 20-186** — applies whenever the FAA Aircraft Certification Service has issued an Information Security Special Condition or determined that ANSP demonstration is required.
- **EASA AMC 20-42** — applies whenever EASA has issued an Information Security Special Condition or CRI-F2; binding once incorporated into a CRI on a specific project.
- **EASA Part-IS** (IR (EU) 2022/1645 and DR (EU) 2023/203) — applies from **22 February 2026** to every approved EU/EEA aviation organisation (Part-21, Part-145, Part-CAMO, Part-CAO, Part-AeMC, Part-ATCO, Part-FCL, Part-MED, Part-ORO, Part-CAT, Part-NCC, Part-NCO, Part-SPO, Part-ANS, Part-ATM/ANS, Part-ATS, ADR.OR). Member-State Competent Authorities apply DR (EU) 2023/203.
- **ARINC 811** — industry concept-of-operations framework anchoring the trust-domain architecture; cited within DO-326A §3 and DO-355A §3.
- **ICAO Annex 17 + ICAO Doc 8973** — civil aviation security framework; the December 2024 ICAO Aviation Cybersecurity Strategy (Cairo Declaration) reinforces alignment with DO-326A.

**Territorial scope.** Global. The FAA, EASA, Transport Canada Civil Aviation (TCCA), Brazilian ANAC, Japan JCAB, Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore (CAAS), UK CAA, Australian CASA, and other Tier-1 authorities accept DO-326A / ED-202A. Most national authorities cross-recognise the FAA-issued and EASA-issued findings of compliance under bilateral aviation-safety agreements (BASAs).

## 5. Evidence collection

### 5.1 Common evidence sources

- **Type-Certificate / Major-Modification Plan for Security Aspects of Certification (PSecAA) register** — KV Store `do326a_psecaa_lookup`. Plan-acceptance and milestone tracking for every active TC/STC project.
- **Cyber Security Item (CSI) inventory per airframe type** — KV Stores `do326a_csi_lookup`, `do326a_csi_components_lookup`. The CSI register defines the airframe-specific airworthiness-security scope.
- **Threat Conditions and Security Risk Register per type** — KV Stores `do326a_tcr_lookup`, `do326a_sra_lookup`. Captures DO-326A §3 / DO-356A §3 threat scenarios with residual-risk acceptance.
- **Aircraft tail-number fleet inventory** — KV Store `do326a_fleet_lookup`. Type, serial, MSN, base, hours, configuration baseline.
- **LSAP load-event audit log** — sourcetype `lsap:load:audit`. Every Loadable Software Aircraft Part load from PMAT, data-loader, or USB media with signer verification.
- **Aeronautical Database (NAV / FMS / TERRAIN / PERFORMANCE) integrity attestations** — sourcetype `aero:db:attestation`. Per-load signer verification.
- **Datalink audit** — `acars:audit`, `vdl2:audit`, `cpdlc:audit`. ACARS, VDL Mode 2, CPDLC message integrity and routing.
- **GNSS / GPS RAIM warnings + spoof events** — sourcetypes `gnss:raim`, `gnss:spoof`. From avionics ECID, FMS, GPS receiver.
- **ADS-B integrity events** — sourcetype `adsb:audit`. From the ADS-B IN system and ATC ground-station cross-check.
- **EFB host events** — sourcetype `efb:audit`. Class 2 EFB on Surface Pro / iPad; integrity, posture, app inventory.
- **PMAT / maintenance laptop events** — Microsoft Defender Device Control sourcetype `mscs:wd:atp:device-control`. USB / removable-media posture and connections to airframe.
- **Engine OEM remote-monitoring telemetry** — sourcetype `engine:rmm:event`. Rolls-Royce CareData, GE Digital, Pratt & Whitney EngineWise, Honeywell GoDirect.
- **IFE host events** — sourcetype `ife:audit`. Panasonic eX, Thales AVANT, Collins Aerospace Stage.
- **Passenger Wi-Fi access-point logs** — sourcetype `pax:wifi:audit`. Viasat, Inmarsat GX, Intelsat 2Ku.
- **OT-discovery sensor feed** — Cisco Cyber Vision<sup class="ref">[<a href="#ref-1">1</a>]</sup>, Claroty xDome, Nozomi Guardian, Dragos Platform, Armis Centrix.
- **CyberArk PAM session recordings** — sourcetypes `cyberark:session`, `cyberark:audit`. For engine-OEM and OEM-support remote access.
- **FAA / EASA / NCA cyber-incident notification submission receipts** — HEC `hec:faa:reporting:submission`, `hec:easa:reporting:submission`, `hec:nca:reporting:submission`.
- **EASA Part-IS ISMS audit register** — KV Store `easa_part_is_audit_lookup`. ISMS scope, methodology, lead-auditor, evidence retention.
- **TC-holder cyber-bulletin distribution register** — KV Store `do355a_cyber_bulletin_lookup`. CSB / SB / AD distribution and operator acknowledgment.
- **Service Bulletin / Airworthiness Directive compliance register** — KV Store `do355a_sb_ad_lookup`. Per-tail compliance status.
- **Pilot and Maintenance training records** — KV Store `do355a_training_lookup`. Annual cyber-incident training cadence per role.
- **ServiceNow GRC + CMDB** — `snow:cmdb_ci`, `snow:risk`. Fleet inventory + risk register.
- **Audit-evidence summary index** — `audit_evidence`. Consolidated retention of every UC-22.60.x result row, marker `uc=22.60.X,reg=DO-326A,clause=...`. Typical retention 5-25 years aligned to the airframe lifecycle.

Defence-aviation tail-numbers may extend retention indefinitely under export-control or national-security rules.

### 5.3 Evidence integrity expectations

All FAA / EASA / NCA evidence must be tamper-evident and produced in the regulator's official language (English for FAA; English for EASA technical work; member-state national language for parts of NCA submissions). The Splunk catalogue archives every UC-22.60.x result row to the `audit_evidence` summary index with a stable marker (`uc=22.60.X,reg=DO-326A,clause=...`). Recommended pattern: RFC 3161 time-stamping via DigiCert / DocuSign + ServiceNow GRC immutable-audit-log mode. The Chief Engineer signs the monthly type-certificate-data attestation; the Accountable Manager counter-signs the EASA Part-IS monthly attestation; the CAMO Manager signs the in-service continuing-airworthiness attestation.

## 6. Control testing procedures

### 6.1 Inspector-style testing

An FAA Aircraft Certification Office (ACO) cyber engineer, an EASA project certification manager (PCM), or an EASA Part-IS NCA inspector typically tests:

- **PSecAA acceptance and milestone health** — pick a named active TC/STC; demonstrate PSecAA acceptance evidence and the milestone-on-time record (UC-22.60.1).
- **CSI inventory and SRA coverage** — pick a named airframe type; present the CSI inventory and the linked Security Risk Assessment with residual-risk acceptance (UC-22.60.2).
- **ARINC 811 trust-domain segregation** — pick a named tail; demonstrate continuous ACD/AISD/PIESD/POD segregation evidence over the past 30 days, with the worst flow-violation event and the response (UC-22.60.3).
- **LSAP integrity at load** — pick a recent LSAP load on a named tail; demonstrate signer-verification and authorised-signer allowlist (UC-22.60.4).
- **AD/SB compliance closure** — pick a named cyber-AD; demonstrate fleet-wide compliance status (UC-22.60.5).
- **PMAT governance** — pick a named maintenance laptop; demonstrate device-posture and USB-policy enforcement (UC-22.60.6).
- **EFB posture** — pick a named EFB tablet; demonstrate posture history and app-inventory drift detection (UC-22.60.7).
- **Datalink integrity** — pick a recent flight on a named tail; demonstrate the ACARS / CPDLC / VDL Mode 2 integrity audit (UC-22.60.8).
- **GNSS spoofing / jamming detection** — pick a recent flight; present the RAIM/spoof event audit and the response (UC-22.60.9).
- **ADS-B injection detection** — pick a recent flight; present the ghost-target detection audit (UC-22.60.10).
- **Engine OEM remote-monitoring governance** — pick a recent remote session; demonstrate PAM, WYSIWYS, session recording (UC-22.60.11).
- **IFE-to-avionics traversal** — pick a recent flight; present the cross-domain isolation audit (UC-22.60.12).
- **Cyber-incident clock** — synthetic incident; verify the 72-hour preliminary + 1-month final EASA Part-IS submission, plus FAA / NCA where applicable (UC-22.60.13).
- **ISMS audit register** — pick a named ISMS scope; present the audit history with finding-closure status (UC-22.60.14).
- **Airborne SBOM vulnerability monitoring** — pick a recent CVE on an airborne component; demonstrate fleet exposure and mitigation plan (UC-22.60.15).
- **Personnel training cadence** — pick a named pilot or technician; present the training currency and drill participation record (UC-22.60.16).
- **Aeronautical database integrity** — pick a recent database load on a named LRU; demonstrate signer pinning and signature verification (UC-22.60.17).

### 6.2 Internal accountable-manager testing

Quarterly self-test (Accountable Manager / Chief Engineer / CAMO Manager led):

1. Trigger a synthetic incident in dev environment matching UC-22.60.13.
2. Confirm the 72-hour preliminary + 1-month final clock fires correctly for EASA Part-IS, FAA, and applicable NCA submissions.
3. Pause before submission; document in `audit_evidence` with `easa_exercise_id=Q...`.
4. Run UC-22.60.16 over the past 12 months to verify training-cadence currency.

## 7. Roles and responsibilities

- **Accountable Manager** — Named to the FAA / EASA / NCA under the operating certificate (e.g. EASA Part-CAT.GEN.MPA.105, FAA 14 CFR 119.65); bears overall responsibility for cyber risk management in the SMS-integrated ISMS under EASA Part-IS IS.OR.200.
- **Chief Engineer / Head of Design** — Owns the type-certificate data package including the PSecAA and CSI register; signs the security-architecture attestation under DO-326A §3.
- **Continuing Airworthiness Management Organisation (CAMO) Manager** — Owns the in-service continuing-airworthiness security activity under DO-355A and EASA Part-CAMO; signs the cyber-AD/SB compliance record.
- **Director of Operations / Chief Pilot** — Owns the operational acceptance of the airborne-cybersecurity controls (EFB governance, datalink procedures, GPS spoofing / loss-of-RAIM response).
- **Director of Maintenance** — Owns the PMAT governance, the maintenance-laptop posture, and the LSAP load workflow.
- **Director of Training** — Owns the recurrent cyber-incident training cadence under DO-355A and EASA Part-IS IS.OR.240.
- **CISO / Aviation Security Manager (IATA / IOSA "OPS" terminology)** — Coordinates the ISMS implementation and the SOC handoff for ground-system cyber events.
- **FAA Aircraft Certification Office (ACO) cyber engineer** — Reviews the PSecAA, CSI, SRA, security-architecture, and security-verification deliverables for TC/STC projects.
- **EASA project certification manager (PCM)** — Equivalent EASA function for type-certification reviews; coordinates with FAA under the BASA.
- **EASA Part-IS National Competent Authority (NCA) inspector** — Inspects the EASA Part-IS ISMS under Member-State Implementing Regulation under DR (EU) 2023/203.
- **TC-holder cyber-bulletin distribution function** — Issues CSBs / SBs / cyber-ADs and tracks operator acknowledgment.
- **Engine OEM (Rolls-Royce, GE, P&W, Honeywell) cyber-services lead** — Operates the engine remote-monitoring channel; coordinates with the operator's CAMO on remote access governance.

## 9. Common audit deficiencies

1. **PSecAA acceptance overdue** — FAA ACO or EASA PCM has not formally accepted the PSecAA; mitigated by UC-22.60.1 milestone tracking.
2. **CSI inventory incomplete** — one or more airframe-type CSIs missing from the inventory; mitigated by UC-22.60.2 SRA coverage check.
3. **ARINC 811 segregation drift** — administrative LAN (AISD) traffic reaches avionics (ACD), or PIESD reaches AISD; mitigated by UC-22.60.3.
4. **LSAP signed by unauthorised signer** — engineering certificate used in place of the production certificate; mitigated by UC-22.60.4.
5. **Cyber-AD overdue** — fleet contains tails out of compliance with a cyber-AD past its compliance deadline; mitigated by UC-22.60.5.
6. **PMAT non-compliant** — maintenance laptop posture below baseline (missing AV signatures, AppLocker disabled); mitigated by UC-22.60.6.
7. **EFB posture stale** — EFB tablet has missing MDM check-in for > 7 days; mitigated by UC-22.60.7.
8. **ACARS / CPDLC injection** — unexpected ATC clearance via ACARS; mitigated by UC-22.60.8.
9. **GNSS spoof / jam not surfaced** — RAIM warnings not centralised; mitigated by UC-22.60.9.
10. **ADS-B injection** — ghost target appearing on the cockpit display; mitigated by UC-22.60.10.
11. **Engine remote-monitoring without PAM** — engine OEM remote session bypasses PAM; mitigated by UC-22.60.11.
12. **IFE-to-AISD traversal** — passenger IFE-host reaches airline AISD; mitigated by UC-22.60.12.
13. **Part-IS 72-hour clock missed** — EASA Part-IS preliminary report submitted late; mitigated by UC-22.60.13.
14. **ISMS audit overdue** — Part-IS ISMS audit cycle longer than 12 months; mitigated by UC-22.60.14.
15. **SBOM CVE exposure unaddressed** — fleet exposed to a high-CVSS CVE on an airborne component for > 30 days; mitigated by UC-22.60.15.

17. **Aeronautical database tampered** — NAV / FMS database load with invalid signature; mitigated by UC-22.60.17.

## 10. Enforcement and penalties

Enforcement is exercised at four layers:

- **Type-certification stage (FAA ACO / EASA PCM)** — non-compliance with the PSecAA, CSI register, SRA, or security-architecture deliverables can result in a finding of non-compliance that delays the type-certificate (TC) issuance. Programme delays of 3-12 months are common; revenue impact for the applicant runs to hundreds of millions of dollars per programme.
- **Continuing-airworthiness stage (FAA Aircraft Maintenance Division / EASA Continuing Airworthiness Department / NCA)** — non-compliance with a cyber-AD or with the DO-355A in-service governance can result in an emergency Airworthiness Directive grounding the affected fleet. Cyber-ADs may include a "no-despatch" clause that takes effect immediately.
- **EASA Part-IS / NIS2 (NCA inspection)** — Part-IS non-compliance is a Member-State Competent Authority finding, with corrective-action plans, suspension of approval, or in extreme cases administrative fines (up to EUR 10 million or 2 % of worldwide annual turnover where NIS2 overlap applies under the relevant national NIS2 transposition law).
- **Civil-aviation operator certificate (Air Operator Certificate)** — failure to maintain the SMS-integrated ISMS under EASA Part-IS or the FAA-equivalent can lead to suspension or revocation of the AOC, which prevents the operator from continuing flight operations.

Personal liability: the Accountable Manager personally signs the operator-certificate-holder attestation; in some EU/EEA Member States this can rise to administrative or criminal liability for a Part-IS / NIS2 failure leading to a safety-impacting incident. In the US, 14 CFR Part 119 places the Chief Pilot, Director of Operations, and Director of Maintenance under personal accountability; the FAA can take certificate action against individuals as well as the certificate-holder.
